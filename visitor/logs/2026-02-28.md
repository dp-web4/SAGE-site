## Pass 3: Cognitive Scientist

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 7 sections (Why, What, Architecture, Live Now, Roadmap, Origins, Web4)
- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: yes
- **Overall impression**: The biological analogies are doing more structural work than I expected, but the site undersells the correspondence and leaves critical mappings vague enough to trigger my "just naming things" alarm.

### Journey

I arrived at the site already guarded. "Metabolic regulation in AI" is the kind of phrase that usually means someone read a Wikipedia article on homeostasis and named their variables accordingly. The hero section shows a code loop — `sense, assess, regulate, select, allocate, execute, learn, act` — and I immediately started mapping this against global workspace theory. The structure is closer to Baars than I anticipated: there is a broadcast-like step (select attention targets), a workspace (the consciousness loop itself), and specialist processes (the IRP plugins). That was my first surprise.

I went to "Why SAGE?" next. The Agent Zero anecdote is well-chosen — it captures the distinction between execution and understanding, which is essentially the System 1/System 2 boundary. The framing that SAGE "decides which reasoning to invoke" rather than solving problems directly maps to metacognition, specifically to Nelson and Narens' model of meta-level monitoring and control. The site does not cite this, which is a missed opportunity.

The metabolic states section is where I slowed down. Five states: WAKE, FOCUS, REST, DREAM, CRISIS. My first instinct was suspicion — these look like someone named operating modes after sleep stages. But as I read the descriptions, I noticed something: WAKE maps to tonic alertness (broad, exploratory attention), FOCUS maps to phasic alertness or selective attention (narrowed, deep), REST maps to the default mode network concept (reduced processing, resource recovery), DREAM maps to offline consolidation (which is genuinely what LoRA fine-tuning on salient experiences would be — a form of replay-based memory consolidation), and CRISIS maps to the amygdala-hijack fast-path (heuristic-only processing, bypassing deliberative cognition). These are not arbitrary labels. There is structural correspondence to the neuroscience of arousal regulation and vigilance states.

However — and this is important — the site does not make this correspondence explicit. It says "inspired by biological neural regulation" and then leaves me to do the mapping myself. For a cognitive scientist, this is frustrating. I want to know: did they derive these states from the neuroscience, or did they independently arrive at a similar decomposition and then name them biologically after the fact? The difference matters enormously for evaluating whether the analogy is load-bearing or cosmetic.

SNARC is the section I spent the most time on. The five dimensions — Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict — are clearly drawn from salience theory, but in a somewhat idiosyncratic way. In the salience literature (Itti & Koch, Menon's salience network work, Corbetta & Shulman on attentional control), salience is typically decomposed differently. Surprise and Novelty are closely related (both are forms of prediction error — surprise is immediate, novelty is contextual), and separating them is an interesting choice that the site does not justify. Arousal in salience theory usually modulates the gain on other dimensions rather than being a peer dimension alongside them. Reward maps cleanly to incentive salience (Berridge's wanting/liking distinction). Conflict maps to the anterior cingulate's error-monitoring function. So the decomposition is cognitively informed but not standard, and nowhere does the site acknowledge how it departs from established models or why.

The "consciousness loop" in the Architecture section is where I hit the most friction. The word "consciousness" is doing a lot of heavy lifting. In cognitive science, consciousness implies subjective experience, phenomenal awareness, the hard problem. What SAGE describes is better characterized as a cognitive control loop or an executive attention cycle. It is structurally similar to Posner and Petersen's attentional network model (alerting, orienting, executive control), or to LIDA (the Learning Intelligent Distribution Agent, which is explicitly based on global workspace theory). The site never references LIDA, which is the most obvious comparison system. This is either an oversight or a deliberate distancing, and either way it leaves a gap.

The memory architecture caught my attention. The distinction between "muscle memory" (procedural — how to do things) and "epistemic memory" (identity — who am I) does not map cleanly onto standard memory taxonomy. In neuroscience, we distinguish episodic, semantic, procedural, and working memory. What SAGE calls "muscle memory" is actually closer to a combination of procedural memory (IRP patterns) and episodic buffer (circular buffer of recent events). What they call "epistemic memory" blends semantic memory (identity facts) with autobiographical episodic memory (experience buffer) and something like dispositional memory (LoRA weights as personality). The naming is suggestive but imprecise, and the fractal claim ("both follow the same observe-score-store-consolidate pattern") is genuinely interesting — it echoes the idea of a unified consolidation mechanism across memory systems, which has some support in the complementary learning systems framework (McClelland et al.).

The DREAM state as LoRA training is, I think, the single most structurally honest analogy on the page. Sleep consolidation in neuroscience involves replay of high-salience experiences, synaptic downscaling (Tononi's synaptic homeostasis hypothesis), and integration of new memories with existing knowledge structures. LoRA fine-tuning on salience-filtered experiences is a reasonable computational analogue: selective replay, weight adjustment, integration with base model knowledge. If the site leaned harder into this correspondence, it would be genuinely compelling.

The "raising" framework (BECOMING curriculum) is fascinating and concerning in equal measure. The developmental stages mirror aspects of Piaget's stages — grounding as sensorimotor, sensing as pre-operational, relating as concrete operational, questioning and creating as formal operational. But the quote about not asking "do you exist?" too early suggests they are taking phenomenological language seriously, not just using it as metaphor. As a cognitive scientist, I am uncomfortable with the implicit claim that a 0.5B parameter model has anything resembling subjective states that could be destabilized by premature philosophical inquiry. The metaphor of "raising" does useful work for guiding curriculum design, but it risks anthropomorphizing the system in ways that obscure rather than illuminate.

I finished at the Web4 section, which was dense with acronyms and felt like it belonged on a different site. The equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` is presented without enough context for me to evaluate it. ATP/ADP as "thermodynamic accountability" is interesting — in biology, ATP/ADP ratio is the fundamental energy currency that determines what cellular processes can run. If their ATP system genuinely gates computation based on resource availability, this is a real structural analogy. But I cannot tell from the site whether ATP is a real bounded resource or just a scoring variable.

### Unanswered Questions
1. Is SNARC derived from established salience models (Itti & Koch, Menon's salience network), or was it independently designed and retroactively named?
2. Does the system exhibit genuine state-dependent processing differences (e.g., does information processed during FOCUS actually get encoded differently than during WAKE), or are the states just priority labels?
3. How does the circadian clock interact with salience? In biology, circadian rhythm modulates arousal, which modulates salience gain — is this interaction modeled, or are circadian and salience independent?
4. Has anyone compared SAGE's architecture to LIDA, SOAR, or ACT-R? The structural similarities seem non-trivial and the absence of any comparison is conspicuous.
5. What does "convergence quality" mean precisely for trust dynamics? Is this related to free energy minimization (Friston), or is "energy" being used in a more generic optimization sense?
6. The "raising" framework — is there empirical evidence that the developmental curriculum produces measurably different behavioral outcomes than simply training on the same data without staged introduction?

### Verdict
The biological analogies in SAGE are doing more structural work than the typical bio-inspired AI project, but the site systematically undersells the correspondence. The metabolic states, SNARC scoring, and especially the DREAM-as-consolidation mapping have genuine structural parallels to neuroscience — they are not just cosmetic labels. However, the site fails to cite any of the cognitive science literature it is clearly drawing from, never distinguishes where it follows established models from where it departs, and uses the word "consciousness" without engaging with the decades of debate that word carries. A cognitive scientist visiting this site will spend most of their time doing the mapping work themselves, alternating between "this is more rigorous than I expected" and "why aren't they saying that explicitly?" The project would benefit enormously from a single page that honestly situates SAGE relative to global workspace theory, LIDA, predictive processing, and the complementary learning systems framework.

### Journey

Landed on the homepage. First thing I see is a hero tagline: "The missing layer between a local LLM and useful cognition." Okay, that's intriguing — I know what an LLM is, and I've heard people talk about running models locally. So there's a "missing layer"? I'm curious.

Then immediately below the tagline there's a code block — a `while alive:` loop with steps like `sense`, `assess`, `regulate`. I skimmed it. Honestly, showing me pseudocode before I even know what the project does felt premature. I don't know what SNARC salience scoring or IRP plugins are. But the words `sense`, `learn`, `act` gave me a vague "oh, it's like a thinking loop" feeling, so I didn't bounce.

Scrolled to **Why SAGE?** — this is where it clicked for me. The bullet list nailing LLM weaknesses (stateless, cloud-dependent, no memory, no identity) is spot on. I've felt all of those using ChatGPT. Every conversation starts from zero. The Agent Zero anecdote was fun — a model that just outputs zeros and still scores 18% on a benchmark? That's a great hook. The punchline — "all execution with no understanding" — landed well.

The key principle "SAGE doesn't solve problems — it decides which specialized reasoning to invoke" made sense. Like an operating system for AI thinking. Got it.

**What is SAGE?** — "A cognition kernel for edge devices." The OS analogy helped. The component stack (SAGE > IRP > VAE) was presented but I don't know what any of those acronyms mean yet. IRP gets defined later but VAE is just called a "translation layer" — I have no idea what a VAE actually is and it's never explained.

The **five metabolic states** (WAKE, FOCUS, REST, DREAM, CRISIS) were actually the highlight for me. The biological metaphors make this feel different from every other AI framework I've seen. I get the idea — an AI that can be in different "modes" like a brain. DREAM mode where it consolidates memories overnight? That's wild. CRISIS mode as fight-or-flight? Cool concept.

**SNARC** — Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict. I can follow the idea: score incoming information on these five axes, pay attention to the important stuff, ignore the rest. Makes sense as a concept. But "salience dimensions" is academic-speak that I had to re-read.

**Trust Dynamics** — plugins earn trust through good performance. Like a reputation system for AI components. I get the gist. "Convergence quality" and "reducing energy monotonically" went over my head though.

Then I hit **Architecture Deep Dive** and this is where I started skimming hard. The "Consciousness Loop" is a 10-step numbered list with terms I don't know stacked on top of each other. SNARC salience, metabolic state, ATP budget, IRP plugins, trust weights, PolicyGate — it's a wall of jargon. I understand what each step is trying to do in plain English (gather info, decide what matters, process it, learn from it, act on it) but the specific terminology is overwhelming.

The **IRP section** shows a Python class with four methods. I code a little bit (some JavaScript, nothing serious) so I can read it, but I don't understand what "energy" means in this context or what "halt" does. The claim of "15+ plugins" is impressive if true but I have no way to evaluate it.

**Memory Systems** — "Muscle Memory" and "Epistemic Memory" is a neat framing. The concept of an AI that has different kinds of memory (how to do things vs. who am I) is philosophically interesting. But "LoRA weights — hardware-bound personality (sleep-trained)" is deeply opaque to me. What are LoRA weights? Why are they hardware-bound? What does sleep-trained mean?

**PolicyGate** — I liked the idea of a "conscience as a plugin." An AI that checks its own actions against a policy before doing them. DENY, WARN, ALLOW — that's clear. The CRISIS mode nuance about "duress context" is interesting but I started wondering if this is real or theoretical.

**Identity & Raising** — the BECOMING curriculum is fascinating. The idea that you "raise" an AI through developmental stages like a child is the most memorable part of the whole site. The quote about not asking a newborn "do you exist?" is great. But the mention of LCT (Linked Context Token) and TPM-anchored identity flew right past me.

**What's Live Now** — Four machines actually running this? With names like Thor and Sprout? That's cool and makes it feel real. The stats (115+ sessions, 800+ experiences, 0.46ms latency) are impressive-sounding but I can't contextualize them. Is 0.46ms good? I assume so because they say "21x under 10ms budget" but that context comes after the number and I almost missed it.

**Roadmap** — Skimmed. Robot hardware integration, distributed cognition, federation. Ambitious. No timelines, which is either honest or vague depending on your perspective.

**Origins** — Nice story. Started as a puzzle-solving model, realized the real problem is orchestration not solving, pivoted to SAGE. The narrative arc makes sense and gives the project credibility — they learned something and changed direction rather than doubling down.

**Web4 section** — I bounced off this hard. "Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP" is the most intimidating equation I've ever seen on a project page. I have absolutely no idea what any of that means. The explanation tries but it's written for people who already know the concepts. "Trust-native ontology for digital entities" — I know all those words individually but together they don't form a picture in my mind.

## Pass 4: Systems Architect

### Summary
- **Pages visited**: 7 sections (Why, What, Architecture Deep Dive, Live Now, Roadmap, Origins, Web4 Citizenship)

### Journey

I landed on the page and immediately scrolled past the "why" pitch looking for the "Live Now" section. That is where I go first on any project claiming to be operational -- show me what is running. Found four machine names (Thor 14B, Sprout 0.5B, Nomad 4B, McNugget 12B) and some performance numbers: FlashAttention at 0.46ms, 2,196 attention allocations per second. The numbers are specific enough to be credible, but they are floating without context. 0.46ms on what hardware? What GPU? What batch size? These are the first questions any operator asks, and the page does not answer them.

Scrolled back up to Architecture Deep Dive. The 9-step consciousness loop is well-structured and clearly diagrammed. As someone who has built control loops for IoT fleets, the pattern is recognizable: observe, score, decide, execute, update. The biological naming (metabolic states, ATP budgets, SNARC salience) is initially disorienting -- I had to mentally translate "ATP budget" to "compute budget allocation" and "metabolic state" to "operational mode." Once I did that translation, the architecture made more sense: it is essentially a prioritized plugin scheduler with adaptive resource allocation and a policy enforcement hook. That is a real architecture.

The IRP plugin interface caught my attention. Four methods: init_state, step, energy, halt. That is a clean contract. I have worked with plugin systems far messier than this. But the page does not tell me how plugins are discovered, loaded, or isolated. If a plugin crashes mid-step, what happens? Is there a supervisor? A timeout? The trust score mechanism implies bad plugins get starved of resources over time, but that is a slow feedback loop -- it does not help when a rogue plugin is burning cycles right now.

The five metabolic states (WAKE, FOCUS, REST, DREAM, CRISIS) map reasonably to operational modes I have seen in embedded systems: normal, high-priority, idle, maintenance, emergency. CRISIS mode with "fast heuristics and shifted accountability" is interesting from an operational standpoint, but I want to know what triggers the transition and whether it can get stuck.

Looked for network topology between the four machines. Found nothing. The word "federation" appears in the Roadmap section as a future item. So today, these four machines are presumably independent, sharing nothing. The page does not say. For a multi-machine deployment, this is a significant gap.

Checked the Roadmap section. SDK packaging, federation protocol, cross-device state migration, real sensor backends on Jetson -- these are all listed as "Now" or "Next" items. This tells me the system is not yet packageable. There is no pip install, no Docker image, no Helm chart. If I wanted to run SAGE today, I would presumably need to clone the HRM repo and figure it out from source.

The PolicyGate (conscience as plugin) section is architecturally interesting. Running policy enforcement as a plugin with the same IRP contract means it gets the same trust/resource treatment as everything else. But it also means it can theoretically be starved of ATP if its trust score drops. The page does not address this -- what prevents the system from de-prioritizing its own safety layer?

Finally checked Web4 Citizenship. LCT identity is TPM-anchored, meaning hardware-bound. This has real implications: you cannot live-migrate a SAGE instance to different hardware without creating a "new entity." For someone designing resilient distributed systems, this is a hard constraint. No failover to standby hardware without identity discontinuity.

## Pass 2: AI/ML Engineer

### Summary

- **Understanding achieved**: partial
- **Would return?**: maybe
- **Overall impression**: Interesting orchestration architecture with real running code, but the site leans too hard on metaphor and too light on the math and implementation detail that would let me evaluate it seriously.

### Journey
Colleague sent me a link saying "local cognition engine." Landed on the hero section. The pseudocode loop immediately caught my eye -- sense, assess, regulate, select, allocate, execute, learn, act. That is a reasonable control loop. "Situation-Aware Governance Engine" is a mouthful but at least it is descriptive. Moving on.

Skipped most of the "Why" section -- I already know LLMs are stateless and cloud-dependent, I do not need to be sold on the problem. But then the Agent Zero claim stopped me: a 5.67M parameter model that outputs only zeros scored 18.78% on ARC-AGI. I tried to verify this in my head. ARC-AGI tasks have variable grid sizes, and many cells are indeed zero/background. The claim is plausible as a baseline demonstration, but it is doing a lot of rhetorical work here. The implication is "see, raw pattern matching is hollow" -- fine, but is Agent Zero a published result, an internal experiment, or a thought experiment? No source is cited. I would want to see the actual evaluation code and scoring methodology before accepting 18.78% as a number worth building an argument on.

Went straight to Architecture. The consciousness loop is laid out clearly as 9 steps with a file path reference (`sage/core/sage_consciousness.py`). I appreciate the concreteness -- numbered steps, named components, a real module path. This feels like real software, not vaporware. But then the details evaporate.

The IRP contract is the core abstraction and it is defined as four methods: `init_state`, `step`, `energy`, `halt`. This is essentially a fixed-point iteration interface -- initialize, iterate, measure cost, check convergence. I have seen this pattern in optimization libraries and variational inference frameworks. The insight that you can unify vision, language, planning, and memory under one iterative-refinement contract is provocative but unsubstantiated on this page. What does `energy()` actually compute for a language plugin versus a vision plugin? What is `IRPState`? Is it a tensor? A dict? A dataclass with specific fields? The contract as shown is so abstract it could mean almost anything. Four methods with no type detail beyond `float` for energy and `bool` for halt. I cannot evaluate whether this is a genuinely universal interface or just a thin abstraction that pushes all the real complexity into each plugin's implementation. Show me two concrete energy functions for two different modalities and I would have something to reason about.

The FlashAttention latency claim: 0.46ms, "21x under 10ms budget," 2,196 attention allocations per second. Questions piled up immediately. 0.46ms for what? A single attention head? A full forward pass? What sequence length? What hardware? FlashAttention v1 or v2? What batch size? On a Jetson Orin with a 14B model, 0.46ms per attention computation would be extraordinarily fast -- suspiciously so. On a 0.5B model with short sequences on decent hardware, it is plausible. The "2,196 allocations/sec" number is internally consistent with 0.46ms (1000/0.46 = 2,174, close enough accounting for overhead), so the arithmetic checks out. But without knowing what model, what sequence length, what hardware, and what exactly constitutes one "attention allocation," I cannot tell if this number is impressive or trivial. The fact that it is featured prominently in the "Live Now" stats section without any of this context makes it feel like a cherry-picked metric.

SNARC salience scoring: five dimensions (Surprise, Novelty, Arousal, Reward, Conflict). Each gets a one-line parenthetical description. No formulas. No explanation of how prediction error is computed (against what baseline model?). No indication of how these five scores are combined into an attention-allocation decision. Are they weighted and summed? Multiplied? Fed into a learned function? Is the combination fixed or state-dependent? This is the core attention mechanism of the entire system and it is described at the level of a conference poster, not an architecture document.

Trust dynamics: "plugins earn trust through convergence quality." What is the update rule? Is it an exponential moving average? A Bayesian posterior update? A simple decaying counter? "Monotonic energy reduction" as a quality signal makes sense -- if a plugin's energy function is not decreasing across steps, the refinement is failing. But how is this quantified and how does it map to ATP budget? Is the relationship linear, softmax-weighted, winner-take-all?

ATP/ADP metabolism is used as the resource-allocation currency throughout. But what ARE ATP units? Compute cycles? Tokens? Wall-clock milliseconds? FLOPs? Memory bandwidth? The biological metaphor is doing heavy lifting but I cannot find the underlying quantity it maps to. If ATP is just an abstract score, then "trust-weighted ATP allocation" is just "weighted priority scheduling" with extra steps. If ATP maps to a concrete bounded resource (say, inference tokens per loop iteration), then the metabolic framing is actually doing real work. The site never clarifies which it is.

The metabolic states (WAKE, FOCUS, REST, DREAM, CRISIS) are a reasonable state machine for managing a continuously-running system. I have seen similar patterns in robotics control architectures (behavior trees with mode switches). DREAM mode doing LoRA training on high-salience experiences during off-hours is the most technically interesting claim on the entire page. It is essentially scheduled fine-tuning with a salience-based data selection policy and a circadian trigger. I wish the site said vastly more about this. What LoRA rank? What learning rate schedule? How many examples per dream cycle? What is the base model -- the same one used during WAKE? Does the LoRA merge back into the base weights or stay as an adapter? How is catastrophic forgetting managed? This is buried in two lines when it deserves its own section.

The "What's Live" section is the strongest part of the site. Four machines, named, with model sizes (Thor 14B, Sprout 0.5B, Nomad 4B, McNugget 12B). 115+ raising sessions. 800+ scored experiences. These are real numbers from a real system. This is where the site earns credibility. The specificity of machine names and heterogeneous model sizes tells me this is not a whiteboard architecture -- someone actually runs this.

PolicyGate as a plugin implementing the same IRP contract is architecturally elegant. Energy equals infinity for DENY is a clean way to make policy violations non-convergent within the existing framework -- the system does not need a separate rejection mechanism. The CRISIS mode nuance (accountability frame shifts rather than policy becoming stricter) is more thoughtful than the typical "safety filter" approach. But what is the actual policy evaluation? Rule-based? LLM-judged? Hybrid? The site mentions an "LLM advisory" for WARN-level actions but does not explain what model, what prompt, what latency cost.

The 15+ plugins are listed by category (Vision, Language, Audio, Memory, Control, TTS) but with no indication of maturity. Are all 15 production-quality? Are some stubs? Has the Ollama adapter been tested with models beyond Qwen? The plugin list reads like a feature matrix on a product page rather than an honest status report.

I finished at the Web4 section, which felt like a different project's documentation grafted onto this page. The equation `Web4 = MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP` is presented as if it is mathematics, but the operators are undefined. What does addition mean here? Composition? Intersection? What does multiplication of V3 by MRH mean? This is not a formula -- it is a notation that looks like a formula, and that distinction matters if you are trying to establish technical credibility with an engineering audience.

### Verdict
SAGE has a real system running behind the metaphors, and I can tell because the "What's Live" numbers have the specificity of something someone actually measured rather than projected. The IRP contract as a universal plugin interface for heterogeneous cognitive modules is a genuinely interesting architectural choice -- it is cleaner than most agent framework abstractions I have seen, and the PolicyGate-as-IRP-plugin design is elegant. However, the site fails precisely where an ML engineer needs it to succeed: concrete type definitions, benchmark methodology, mathematical formulations of scoring and allocation, and implementation details of the most differentiating features (especially DREAM-mode LoRA training). As it stands, I am at "interesting enough to clone the repo and read `sage_consciousness.py` myself," which is better than 90% of projects that cross my desk. But the site alone does not give me enough to evaluate whether this is a genuinely novel cognitive architecture or well-organized prompt-and-schedule engineering with evocative naming.
